    Topic 1.4 - Ethics of Business Analytics¶

    1. Key Ethical Aspects of Business Analytics¶

    Business analysts face ethical considerations in every decision:

    • These decisions include:

      • What data to collect

      • How to analyze it

      • How to act on it

    • More powerful tools to collect and interpret data means greater responsibility

    Three key ethical concerns:

    • Avoid bias:

      • Algorithms and models can reflect and even amplify existing biases in the data

      • For example, a hiring model trained on past decisions may replicate patterns of discrimination unless actively corrected

      • Ethical analytics involves detecting, understanding, and mitigating such bias

    • Protect privacy.

      • Businesses must handle customer, employee, and user data with care - more data comes more risk

      • Business should comply with legal requirements, e.g., GDPR or Australia’s Privacy Act

      • Business should consider whether data is used in ways that stakeholders would regard as reasonable and respectful - go beyond compliance

    • Promote fairness

      • Decisions driven by analytics can have real consequences, e.g., who gets a loan, a promotion, or a targeted offer

      • Ethical analytics means:

        • Being transparent about how decisions are made

        • Striving to ensure that outcomes are just and inclusive

    2. Importance of Ethical Consideration¶

    Ethical decision-making is not an optional add-on, but a core part of responsible business practice:

    • Firms that ignore ethical responsibilities may face backlash, regulatory penalties, and long-term reputational damage
    • Ethical analytics goes beyond risk avoidance by creating positive opportunities to build trust, foster loyalty, and support responsible business practices
    • When done well, business analytics enables firms to grow in ways that are efficient, sustainable, and fair

    Ethical thinking doesn’t happen at the end of an analysis:

    • It must be embedded at every stage, from how we frame questions to how we communicate results

    • This applies in the context of data collection, machine learning, and A/B testing
